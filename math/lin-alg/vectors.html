Vectors and vector spaces

Vectors are objects that can be:

  1. Added together

  2. Stretched by some factor

The operation of adding two vectors is called—unsuprisingly—vector addition, and is denoted with the usual +++ sign. The “stretching” operation is called scalar multiplication and is denoted with a ⋅\cdot⋅ or juxtaposition, just like normal multiplication. So we need two sets in order to define a “vector space”: the set of vectors, and the set of numbers used to stretch the vectors. For reasons which will be made clear below, the “stretching” set must be a field.

These sets (which we'll call VVV and FFF) and the operations defined on them must respect a number of properties. To keep the rules simple, the names uuu, vvv, and www refer to vectors, and aaa and bbb refer to scalars (elements of FFF). All names are implicitly universally quantified.

  1. Vector addition must be associative and commutative:

    (u+v)+w=u+(v+w)u+v=v+u\begin{align*} (u + v) + w &= u + (v + w) \\ u + v &= v + u \end{align*}(u+v)+wu+v​=u+(v+w)=v+u​

  2. There must be some vector 0\mathbf{0}0 that acts as identity for addition:

    ∃0∈V:0+v=v\exists \mathbf{0} \in V : \mathbf{0} + v = v∃0∈V:0+v=v

  3. Each vector has an additive inverse:

    ∀v∈V,∃v′∈V:v+v′=0\forall v \in V, \exists v' \in V : v + v' = \mathbf{0}∀v∈V,∃v′∈V:v+v′=0

  4. Scaling a vector by 111 leaves it unchanged:

    1⋅v=v1 \cdot v = v1⋅v=v

  5. Field multiplication and scalar multiplication need to associate:

    (ab)⋅v=a⋅(b⋅v)(a b) \cdot v = a \cdot (b \cdot v)(ab)⋅v=a⋅(b⋅v)

  6. Finally, two distributive laws need to hold:

    a⋅(u+v)=a⋅u+a⋅v(a+b)⋅v=a⋅v+b⋅v\begin{align*} a \cdot (u + v) = a \cdot u + a \cdot v \\ (a + b) \cdot v = a \cdot v + b \cdot v \end{align*}a⋅(u+v)=a⋅u+a⋅v(a+b)⋅v=a⋅v+b⋅v​

That's a lot of rules, but they can be nicely divided into two groups:

  • Rules 1, 2, and 3 exist to ensure that vector addition “makes sense”1

  • Rules 4, 5, and 6 ensure that scalar multiplication makes sense

1.

Specifically, they require that VVV is an abelian group.
