Linear maps

Linear maps are special

I'm thinking of a function f:R2→R2f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2f:R2→R2. There are a lot of functions from R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 to R2\mathbb{R}^2R2, so I'll give you a hint: f(1,0)=(2,−1)f(1, 0) = (2, -1)f(1,0)=(2,−1) and f(0,1)=(5,3)f(0, 1) = (5, 3)f(0,1)=(5,3). Any hint is better than no hint, but it still leaves an uncountable ocean of possibilities. Even if I tell you that fff is continuous, or differentiable, or twice-differentiable, or smooth, or a polynomial, you'll remain very much in the dark. But if I tell you that fff is linear, you know everything there is to know about it.

Linear maps

We'll just focus on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn in this note, although most of what we'll say applies to any finite-dimensional vector space.

A function L:Rm→RnL : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^nL:Rm→Rn is linear iff

  1. L(x+y)=L(x)+L(y)L(x + y) = L(x) + L(y)L(x+y)=L(x)+L(y) for all x,y∈Rmx, y \in \mathbb{R}^mx,y∈Rm

  2. L(λx)=λL(x)L(\lambda x) = \lambda L(x)L(λx)=λL(x) for all x∈Rmx \in \mathbb{R}^mx∈Rm and λ∈R\lambda \in \mathbb{R}λ∈R.

An immediate, but perhaps not obvious consequence of these properties is this:

LLL's behavior is entirely determined by how it transforms a basis of Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm.

This is the special quality alluded to in the introduction: once you know what LLL does to a basis of its domain, you know how it transforms every point in the domain.

Proof: let w∈Rmw \in \mathbb{R}^mw∈Rm be any point in LLL's domain, let u1,…,um∈Rmu_1, \ldots, u_m \in \mathbb{R}^mu1​,…,um​∈Rm be a basis for Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm, and v1,…,vn∈Rnv_1, \ldots, v_n \in \mathbb{R}^nv1​,…,vn​∈Rn be a basis for Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn. Because u1,…,umu_1, \ldots, u_mu1​,…,um​ form a basis for the domain, www can be expressed as a linear combination of them:

w=c1u1+c2u2+⋯+cmumw = c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2 + \cdots + c_m u_mw=c1​u1​+c2​u2​+⋯+cm​um​

Then

L(w)=L(c1u1+c2u2+⋯+cmum)=L(c1u1)+L(c2u2)+⋯+L(cmum)=c1L(u1)+c2L(u2)+⋯+cmL(um)\begin{align*} L(w) &= L(c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2 + \cdots + c_m u_m) \\ &= L(c_1 u_1) + L(c_2 u_2) + \cdots + L(c_m u_m) \\ &= c_1 L(u_1) + c_2 L(u_2) + \cdots + c_m L(u_m) \end{align*}L(w)​=L(c1​u1​+c2​u2​+⋯+cm​um​)=L(c1​u1​)+L(c2​u2​)+⋯+L(cm​um​)=c1​L(u1​)+c2​L(u2​)+⋯+cm​L(um​)​

This is just another way of saying that L(w)L(w)L(w) is a linear combination of the images of the basis vectors (and this linear combination has the exact same coefficients).

Matrices

Since each L(uk)L(u_k)L(uk​) lives in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, we can represent it as a linear combination of v1,…,vnv_1, \ldots, v_nv1​,…,vn​. That is,

L(u1)=a11v1+a21v2+⋯+an1vnL(u2)=a12v1+a22v2+⋯+an2vn⋮L(um)=a1mv1+a2mv2+⋯+anmvn\begin{align*} L(u_1) &= a_{11} v_1 + a_{21} v_2 + \cdots + a_{n1} v_n \\ L(u_2) &= a_{12} v_1 + a_{22} v_2 + \cdots + a_{n2} v_n \\ \vdots \\ L(u_m) &= a_{1m} v_1 + a_{2m} v_2 + \cdots + a_{nm} v_n \end{align*}L(u1​)L(u2​)⋮L(um​)​=a11​v1​+a21​v2​+⋯+an1​vn​=a12​v1​+a22​v2​+⋯+an2​vn​=a1m​v1​+a2m​v2​+⋯+anm​vn​​

So a matrix is nothing more than a compact representation of a linear transform. Once a basis for the domain and a basis for the codomain have been selected, every linear transform corresponds to a matrix, and every matrix to a linear transform.

Applying the linear transform to a vector www is equivalent to multiplying the matrix times the representation of www in terms of the matrix's “domain basis”.

It's also clear that the columns of the matrix are the images of the domain basis under LLL.
