Quotient spaces

Forming the quotient of a set XXX by an equivalence relation ∼\sim∼ is a ubiquitous operation in mathematics. Unsurprisingly, there's a natural way to construct a topology on the quotient given a topology on the original set. The result is called the quotient space.

The canonical map

Given a set XXX, a topology T\mathcal{T}T on XXX, and an equivalence relation ∼\sim∼ on XXX, the canonical map q:X→X/ ⁣∼q : X \to X/\!\simq:X→X/∼ maps every element in XXX to its equivalence class:

Examples

What's the point of all this?

The möbius strip

Equivalence relations give us a way of saying that certain points are the same as others. This allows us to construct new topological spaces by “gluing” points together in existing spaces.

For example, we can construct a topological space equivalent2 to the möbius strip below:

by gluing the lefthand edge of this rectangle to its righthand edge, after giving it a half-rotation:

The rectangle is the subset [0,1]×(−1,1)⊆R2[0, 1] \times (-1, 1) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2[0,1]×(−1,1)⊆R2. “Gluing instructions” are provided by the arrows on the left and righthand sides of the rectangle: the two edges should be identified in such a way that the arrows point in the same direction. Specifically, a point of the form (0,s)(0, s)(0,s) should be glued to (1,−s)(1, -s)(1,−s). How do we “glue” two points mathematically? We define an equivalence relation in which they're related.

In our case, that's the smallest equivalence relation ∼\sim∼ containing all points in [0,1]×(−1,1)[0, 1] \times (-1, 1)[0,1]×(−1,1) in which (0,s)∼(1,−s)(0, s) \sim (1, -s)(0,s)∼(1,−s), for all s∈(−1,1)s \in (-1, 1)s∈(−1,1). So, for instance, (12,π4)∼(12,π4)\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\pi}{4}\right) \sim \left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\pi}{4}\right)(21​,4π​)∼(21​,4π​), but also (0,23)∼(1,−23)\left(0, \frac{2}{3}\right) \sim \left(1, -\frac{2}{3}\right)(0,32​)∼(1,−32​), and (1,−23)∼(0,23)\left(1, -\frac{2}{3}\right) \sim \left(0, \frac{2}{3}\right)(1,−32​)∼(0,32​), (0,23)∼(0,23)\left(0, \frac{2}{3}\right) \sim \left(0, \frac{2}{3}\right)(0,32​)∼(0,32​), etc.

With the equivalence relation ∼\sim∼ in hand, the quotient topology w.r.t. ∼\sim∼ of the usual topology on [0,1]×(−1,1)[0, 1] \times (-1, 1)[0,1]×(−1,1) behaves like the möbius strip.

Let's define R=[0,1]×(−1,1)R = [0, 1] \times (-1, 1)R=[0,1]×(−1,1). To illustrate this, consider the set {(x,y)∈R∣x2+y2<14}\{ (x, y) \in R \mid \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} < \frac{1}{4} \}{(x,y)∈R∣x2+y2​<41​}. This is just B14(0,0)B_\frac{1}{4}(0, 0)B41​​(0,0) in RRR. So unsurprisingly, this set is open in RRR under the usual topology:

Even though (0,0)(0, 0)(0,0) is “on the edge” of this set, it's open by definition: for every point in the set, there's some open ball around the point that's completely contained in the set. This works because the open balls look like half-circles.
