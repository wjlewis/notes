Hausdorff spaces

It's possible to “port” convergence to general topological spaces, but not without losing some of the nice features it enjoys in metric spaces. Namely, we lose the uniqueness of limits. We can recover this notion (which is important for doing calculus on topological spaces) by constraining our topological spaces slightly. These are Hausdorff spaces.

Convergence in a general topological space

More colloquially, a sequence converges to lll if, given any open neighborhood UUU of lll, there's a “tail” of the sequence entirely within UUU (after some index, the rest of the sequence is in UUU).

Hausdorff spaces

A topological space ⟨X,T⟩\langle X, \mathcal{T} \rangle⟨X,T⟩ is called Hausdorff if all points in XXX can be “separated”. Specifically, for any x,y∈Xx, y \in Xx,y∈X, where x≠yx \ne yx=y, there exist Ux,Uy∈TU_x, U_y \in \mathcal{T}Ux​,Uy​∈T such that x∈Uxx \in U_xx∈Ux​ and y∈Uyy \in U_yy∈Uy​, but Ux∩Uy=∅U_x \cap U_y = \emptysetUx​∩Uy​=∅.
