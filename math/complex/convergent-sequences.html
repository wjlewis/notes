A complex sequence converges if and only if its component sequences converge, and the limits are “where you expect”. That is, suppose σ:N→C\sigma : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{C}σ:N→C and τ,υ:N→R\tau, \upsilon : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R}τ,υ:N→R so that σn=τn+iυn\sigma_n = \tau_n + i\upsilon_nσn​=τn​+iυn​. Then σ→a+ib\sigma \to a + ibσ→a+ib if and only if τ→a\tau \to aτ→a and υ→b\upsilon \to bυ→b. This is particularly useful: to show that a complex sequence converges, it's enough to show that its components converge (in which case the limit is where you expect); we can also show that a sequence diverges by showing that one of its components does so.
