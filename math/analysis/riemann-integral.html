The Riemann integral

Differentiation lets us to do the seemingly impossible: to compute the slope of a function at a single point. Integration will allow us to perform an equally impressive trick: to calculate the area bounded by a function. Not only is this intellectually satisfying, it opens up new avenues in physics, probability, and other fields.

Integrating by hand

In order to motivate the definition to come, and to gain a little appreciation for the task at hand, let's try integrating some simple functions “by hand”. First, we'll try and compute

∫01x2 dx\int_0^1 x^2 \, dx∫01​x2dx

by hand1.

This is the area beneath the parabola f(x)=x2f(x) = x^2f(x)=x2 between x=0x = 0x=0 and x=1x = 1x=1. Unlike a triangle, whose area can be rather easily obtained through a geometric argument, it's not at all clear if the area beneath a parabola can be found through any means.

Instead of approaching the problem geometrically, let's try something more in line with the rest of analysis: we'll try approximating the area using shapes with known areas, and then look at how this approximate area behaves in the limit. To keep things simple, let's cut our interval into nnn equal pieces, and in each subinterval construct a rectangle whose height is equal to the function's value at the subinterval's right endpoint; that is, the kth rectangle has a base of length 1n\frac{1}{n}n1​ and a height of (kn)2\left(\frac{k}{n}\right)^2(nk​)2. Then the total approximate area is

∑k=1nk2=n33+n22+n6\sum_{k = 1}^n k^2 = \frac{n^3}{3} + \frac{n^2}{2} + \frac{n}{6}k=1∑n​k2=3n3​+2n2​+6n​

U(n)=13+12n+16n2U(n) = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2n} + \frac{1}{6n^2}U(n)=31​+2n1​+6n21​

lim⁡n→∞U(n)=13\lim_{n \to \infty} U(n) = \frac{1}{3}n→∞lim​U(n)=31​

Is this truly the area beneath f(x)=x2f(x) = x^2f(x)=x2 between 000 and 111? On the one hand, as nnn increase, the approximations get better and better. On the other hand, they were always overestimates. Let's see what happens if we approximate with underestimates instead. In this case, the sum is

A slightly different expression, but just as before

lim⁡n→∞L(n)=13\lim_{n \to \infty} L(n) = \frac{1}{3}n→∞lim​L(n)=31​

We approximated the area twice: first with consistent overestimates, then with consistent underestimates; and in both cases the approximations converged to 13\frac{1}{3}31​ as we increased their “granularity”. We can feel fairly confident that the value of the integral is, in fact, 13\frac{1}{3}31​.

As a final exercise, let's see what happens if we “parameterize” the integral:

∫abx2 dx\int_a^b x^2 \, dx∫ab​x2dx

Curiously enough, this is what we get if we subtract the value of an antiderivative of x2x^2x2 at aaa from its value at bbb. This isn't a coincidence, but is instead at the heart of the fundamental theorem of calculus.

Overview

Our goal is to find some way to compute areas bounded by functions. We'll first define what we mean by an “integral”, and prove some results that allow us to determine if a function is integrable. Critically, this won't provide us with any tools for actually calculating integrals. Only then will we discover the fundamental theorem of calculus, part of which gives us a way to compute integrals for suitable functions.

A little note on notation: we'll develop some theory related to integrating functions, but then—without warning—we'll use notation that only ever mentions expressions (as we've done above). This is the same thing that happens with differentiation. We can think of

∫abexpr dx\int_a^b \mathrm{expr} \, dx∫ab​exprdx

∫abx↦eval(expr,x)\int_a^b x \mapsto \mathrm{eval}(\mathrm{expr}, x)∫ab​x↦eval(expr,x)

∫01x↦x2+3x\int_0^1 x \mapsto x^2 + 3x∫01​x↦x2+3x
