The gradient

The gradient is a vector indicating the direction of greatest/fastest increase of a scalar-valued function. More specifically, if f:Rm→Rf : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}f:Rm→R is differentiable at some point x0∈Rm\mathbf{x_0} \in \mathbb{R}^mx0​∈Rm, the gradient ∇f\nabla f∇f indicates the direction in which fff increase most steeply at x0\mathbf{x_0}x0​.

Moreover, the gradient describes a “best linear approximation” of fff at x0\mathbf{x_0}x0​ and, as such, it's a generalization of the derivative. In this note, we'll derive the gradient by attempting to calculate this linear approximation.

Extending our notion of differentiability

Let's cavalierly extend our definition of differentiability and the derivative to functions with higher-dimensional domains (Rm→R\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}Rm→R). That is, we'll say a function is differentiable at x0∈Rm\mathbf{x_0} \in \mathbb{R}^mx0​∈Rm iff there exists l,φ:Rm→Rl, \varphi : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}l,φ:Rm→R such that

f(x)=f(x0)+l(x)(x−x0)+φ(x)f(\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{x_0}) + l(\mathbf{x})(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x_0}) + \varphi(\mathbf{x})f(x)=f(x0​)+l(x)(x−x0​)+φ(x)

where lll is linear and

lim⁡x→x0φ(x)∥x−x0∥=0\lim_{\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{x_0}} \frac{\varphi(\mathbf{x})}{\lVert \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x_0} \rVert} = 0x→x0​lim​∥x−x0​∥φ(x)​=0

If a function fff is differentiable at x0\mathbf{x_0}x0​, then its derivative at x0\mathbf{x_0}x0​ is the linear transform lll.

Coordinates

The definition above is “coordinate-free”, dealing only with abstract vectors, linear transforms, etc. To actually compute the quantities, we need to select a basis for Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm and R\mathbb{R}R. Having done so, we can represent lll as a matrix multiplication:

l(x)=Mxl(\mathbf{x}) = M \mathbf{x}l(x)=Mx

The (transpose of the) matrix of the linear transformation lll is what we call the gradient of fff at x0\mathbf{x_0}x0​.

Let's see how this works for the standard basis in two dimensions. Suppose f:R2→Rf : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}f:R2→R and (x0,y0)∈R2(x_0, y_0) \in \mathbb{R}^2(x0​,y0​)∈R2. We're looking for suitable scalars mxm_xmx​ and mym_ymy​ such that

But f(x0,y0)+mx(x−x0)+my(y−y0)f(x_0, y_0) + m_x(x - x_0) + m_y(y - y_0)f(x0​,y0​)+mx​(x−x0​)+my​(y−y0​) is just the equation of a plane that intersects fff at (x0,y0)(x_0, y_0)(x0​,y0​), with a slope mxm_xmx​ in the xxx direction, and mym_ymy​ in the yyy direction. If we wish for this plane to be tangent to fff at (x0,y0)(x_0, y_0)(x0​,y0​), then the slopes ought to match the partial derivatives of fff at (x0,y0)(x_0, y_0)(x0​,y0​).
